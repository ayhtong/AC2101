When Accounting Fails to Warn Us

A reading guide to fair value accounting and financial crises — exploring relevance, faithful representation, and timeliness through the IFRS Conceptual Framework.

Before You Read

Keep These Definitions in Front of You

The article's argument turns on three IFRS qualitative characteristics. Make sure you are clear on them before reading a single page.

The Guiding Question

Between amortised cost and fair value, which better satisfies the IFRS Framework's two fundamental qualitative characteristics — relevance and faithful representation?

🎯

Relevance

Information must have predictive value and confirmatory value — helping users form expectations about the future and verify past judgements. Information that arrives too late to act on has failed this test.

⚖️

Faithful Representation

Information must be complete, neutral, and free from error. It should represent what it claims to represent — without bias, omission, or systematic distortion in one direction.

⏱️

Timeliness

An enhancing characteristic under IFRS. Information must reach decision-makers before it loses its capacity to influence decisions. Think of it as what makes relevant information actually useful in practice.

Three Crises · Pages 411–414

Three Crises, One Repeating Pattern

Read all three crisis case studies looking for the same structure each time. The pattern reveals something structural about amortised cost — not just a run of bad luck.

01
U.S. Savings & Loan Crisis
Interest Rate Risk

As interest rates rose sharply, the value of banks' long-term fixed-rate loans fell. Amortised cost recorded nothing and loans stayed at original value. By the time losses were recognised, more than 1,000 banks had already failed, costing taxpayers an estimated $124 billion.

02
Japan's Lost Decade
Credit Risk

Real estate values collapsed but banks were allowed to delay recognising loan losses. The result: "zombie banks" that looked solvent on paper but were economically hollow. They were kept alive by government capital and continuing to lend badly. Estimated cost to the economy is up to 100 trillion yen, 20% of GDP.

03
2008 U.S. Financial Crisis
Credit + Interest Rate Risk

120 failed banks continue to meet "adequately capitalised" standards just four to six months before collapse. Capital ratios only slipped into problematic "undercapitalised" category in the single quarter before failure, which was too late for anyone to act. Actual losses that resulted were more than 6 times what banks had reported.

The repeating pattern — all three crises
Risk builds in loan portfolios → Amortised cost does not reflect it → Management delays recognition → Delay makes damage far worse
Passage to mark
"Historical cost accounting and consistent underestimation of losses on loan portfolios provided little warning of how much rising interest rates and falling real estate prices were impacting the value of assets, liabilities, and the thrifts' overall financial positions."
Linsmeier (2011), p. 411
Reflective Questions
💬 On Relevance & Timeliness

In all three crises, banks looked healthy on paper until just months before they collapsed. Under amortised cost, managers decide when a loss is "probable" enough to record — and recording a loss makes a bank look weaker. Why might managers want to delay recording a loss? If they do delay, what does that tell us about whether amortised cost can ever deliver timely, decision-useful information?

💬 On Faithful Representation

Amortised cost is based on the original transaction price — verifiable and straightforward. That sounds like a faithful picture of reality. But the IFRS Framework requires information to be complete. If the balance sheet leaves out the fact that loans have fallen in value due to rising credit and interest rate risk, is a picture that is technically accurate but missing important information truly a faithful representation?

Research Evidence · Pages 414–415 including Footnote 3

The Research Evidence

Two academic studies make the empirical case for fair value.

Blankespoor et al. (2010)

Fair Value and Credit Risk

6 times more
correlated with the TED spread than any other accounting model

Bank leverage measured using full fair value measurement tracked real-time credit risk signals far more closely than using measures based on historical costs, current accounting rules, or regulatory capital requirements. Notably, Tier 1 regulatory capital, which regulators rely on most, was the least correlated of all.

Hodder et al. (2006)

Fair Value and Interest Rate Risk

Significantly
more informative
about interest rate risk than current GAAP measures

Income volatility under full fair value measurement better captured a bank's exposure to changing interest rates (i.e., interest rate risk) than measures produced under current reporting standards.

Passage to mark
"Bank leverage measured under a full fair value system is at least six times more highly correlated with the TED spread than is leverage measured under any other accounting model."
Linsmeier (2011), p. 414 — citing Blankespoor et al. (2010)
Reflective Questions
💬 On Relevance

The evidence shows fair value tracks credit and interest rate risk far more closely than amortised cost. However, note that the TED spread, which is a market signal, was already warning about banking sector problems in 2007, well before any financial statements picked it up. If the market already knew the problem, will more fair value accounting actually provide any additional information? Could there be other users who may still benefit from having fair value information in financial statements?

💬 On Faithful Representation

Fair value sounds like a more relevant measure of what assets are worth today. But unlike traded shares, most bank loans do not have an actively traded market price. Banks would need to estimate fair value using their own assumptions and models. As such, would the resulting measures be faithfully represented?

The Trade-off and the Proposed Solution

Relevance versus faithful representation.

Amortised Cost

Transaction-based · Verifiable

✅ Strength
Grounded in an observable transaction price — verifiable, not dependent on models, free from estimation error
✅ Strength
Shows the contractual cash flows the bank expects to collect
❌ Weakness
No mechanism for capturing changing interest rates or deteriorating credit quality
❌ Weakness
Relies on management judgment to trigger loss recognition — consistently too late to be decision-useful

Fair Value

Market-based · Timely

✅ Strength
Reflects current market conditions — timely, risk-sensitive, and predictive of credit and interest rate exposure
✅ Strength
Shows what the market currently thinks the bank's instruments are worth
❌ Weakness
For illiquid loans with no market price, relies on management's own model-based estimates
❌ Weakness
Estimation uncertainty challenges the neutrality and freedom-from-error requirements of faithful representation
Passage to mark — the proposed solution
"This dual presentation in financial statements — which some investors have asked for — would ensure that both relevant measures are given adequate attention by banks and their auditors."
Linsmeier (2011), p. 415
The Dual Presentation — what each measure gives users
Relevance

The fair value measures shows current market-based risk exposure and gives early warning of credit and interest rate deterioration as it happens and not months later.

Faithful Rep.

The amortised cost measures is grounded in a verifiable transaction price and shows expected contractual cash flows, which satisfies the completeness and neutrality requirements.

The Gap

The difference between the two measures captures how far current economic conditions have moved from the original terms of the instrument, which is a third piece of information neither measure alone provides.

Terminology note. This article uses "reliability" where current IFRS uses "faithful representation." They are related but not identical — faithful representation is broader, additionally requiring completeness and neutrality. Translate accordingly when reading. This distinction comes up regularly in examinations.
