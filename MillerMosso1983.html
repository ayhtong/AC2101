The Core Framework

Start Here: What Is Measurement?

Before any figure makes sense, you must understand the three building blocks that sit behind every number in a set of financial statements.

Core Definition — Miller & Mosso (1983)

Measurement is the assignment of numerals to represent the magnitude of an attribute of a phenomenon.

🏢

Phenomenon (P)

The object being measured — a person, an asset, an inventory item, or a business enterprise. The IFRS Conceptual Framework calls these elements of financial statements.

🎯

Attribute (A)

The characteristic of interest — historical cost, fair value, or present value. The IFRS Conceptual Framework identifies multiple measurement bases for different elements.

🔢

Magnitude

The numerical result — e.g., $10,540 or 8 feet. Using numerals increases the precision of the information communicated to decision-makers.

Three Dangers of Substitution

Miller & Mosso warn that unchecked estimation leads to one of these pitfalls — each relevant to how IFRS standards evolve.

01
Practice Is Ambiguous

Observing what preparers currently do cannot confirm whether the substitute or the relevant attribute/phenomenon is actually being measured. The IFRS CF explicitly requires going beyond current practice.

02
Substitutes Become Reality

Over time, the substitute may be treated as the relevant phenomenon or attribute itself. This underlies debates suchas whether historical cost truly represents the economic substance of an asset.

03
Methods Are Overextended

A useful estimation method in one context is wrongly applied to others — there is a need to select the most appropriate estimation or valuation technique for each asset class.

Discounted present value
Item purchased on deferred payment. The present value of the promised future cash payment substitutes for the past cash price. The present value does not become the relevant attribute — it merely approximates it.

Key insight for IAS 2 Inventory: The further right and down you move in Figure 2, the greater the difference between the measured amount and the true cash purchase price. For example, allocated overheads (Case 4) carry no inherent relevance to the past purchase price — they are only acceptable if the allocation reliably approximates what an outright cash purchase would have cost.

Comparing Figures 2 and 3: The four-case matrix structure is identical — only the relevant attribute changes (past price → current price). This is the article's central demonstration: the same estimation logic, and the same risks of substitution, apply regardless of the measurement basis chosen.

Key Principles

What Makes a Measure Useful?

IFRS CF 2018 · Chapter 2

Relevance

Information is relevant when it is capable of making a difference to users' decisions — through predictive value, confirmatory value, or both. For example, a perfectly accurate measure of the wrong attribute is completely irrelevant.

IFRS CF 2018 · Chapter 2

Faithful Representation

To be useful, information must faithfully represent the phenomena it purports to depict. It must be complete, neutral, and free from error. This is the IFRS counterpart to Miller & Mosso's reliability criterion — the measure must correspond to the actual magnitude of the selected attribute.

IFRS 13 · Para. 61–90

The Link to Fair Value Measurement and Hierarchy

IFRS 13 requires entities to maximise observable inputs and minimise unobservable inputs and to indicate the three-level fair value measurement hierarchy (i.e., Levels 1 to 3). Only Case 1 is likely to be a Level 1 measure. Cases 2 to 4 are Level 2 or 3 measures as substitution and estimation are involved (e.g., substitute phenomenon and/or attribute). IFRS 13 mandates additional disclosures for a Level 3 measure precisely because substitution carries greater estimation risk.

How to Apply It

5-Step Measurement Checklist

You can use this process to assess an accounting measurement issue.

  1. 1
    Name the relevant phenomenon (P₁) What exact object are you measuring? An inventory item, a financial instrument, an investment property? The IFRS CF calls these elements — assets, liabilities, equity, income, expenses.
  2. 2
    Name the relevant attribute (A₁) What characteristic/attribute matters for the decision? For examples, historical cost (IAS 2, IAS 16), fair value (IFRS 13, IFRS 9), value in use (IAS 36), or fulfilment value (IFRS 17)? The IFRS CF Chapter 6 maps each measurement base to its decision-usefulness.
  3. 3
    Determine whether the relevant attribute (A₁) of the relevant phenomenon (P₁) can be directly observed? If yes, direct observation (Case 1) and a Level 1 measurement is likely. If no, estimation is involved (Cases 2 to 4) and identifying the most reliable substitutes is required.
  4. 4
    Identify what needs to be substituted Substitute attribute only (Case 2)? Substitute phenomenon only (Case 3)? Or both (Case 4)?
  5. 5
    If substitution is required, evaluate the reliability and relevance of the substitute Does the substitute attribute and phenomenon reliably approximate A₁ and P₁ respectively? Is the resulting measure relevant and useful for decision-making?
"It is not valid to conclude from observations of practice that past prices are relevant because they are used. Similarly, it is not valid to conclude that current prices are not relevant because they are not as widely reported."
— Miller & Mosso (1983), p. 35

Measurement Is Not What It Seems

Current accounting practice may provide ambiguous evidence. Always ask what is truly relevant, what is truly being measured — and how far the substitute strays from the truth. Measurment issues in IFRS standards are centered on this discipline.
