The Gain That Isn't Good News

A reading guide to fair value accounting for liabilities: why a worsening credit rating produces a reported gain, why over 70% of CPAs get it backwards, and what SFRS(I) 9 did about it.

Before You Read

One Fact to Fix in Your Head First

The entire paper hinges on this single relationship. Toggle it below until it feels automatic, nothing else in the reading will make sense until it does.

Own Credit Risk
↓ Deteriorates
→
Income Statement Effect
Gain Recognized
As the company's own creditworthiness falls, the market value of its liabilities falls too, since the company could theoretically settle its debt for less than face value. A fall in what you owe is, mechanically, a gain. The company is doing worse, and the income statement says "gain."
Feels backwards: that's the whole paper
📉

Why It Happens

Fair value of a liability moves inversely with the issuer's credit standing, a core mechanic of discounting at a market rate that includes the issuer's own risk premium.

🤔

Why It's Confusing

Users associate "gain" with good news from a lifetime of exposure to typical gains. The label triggers the wrong prior, regardless of the correct underlying economics.

📘

Why It Matters Now

SFRS(I) 9 (para. 5.7.7 to 5.7.9) now routes the own-credit-risk portion of a fair value liability change to OCI rather than profit or loss, a direct response to this exact problem.

Three Questions the Paper Asks

1

Do financial-statement users misinterpret fair value gains and losses on liabilities as signals about the company's credit risk?

2

Do relational disclosures (footnotes that explicitly state the direction of the credit risk change) improve users' interpretations?

3

Are relational disclosures more effective for gains than for losses, or equally effective either way?

Motivation & Hypotheses Development · pp. 127–129 · ~7 min

Building the Argument, One Hypothesis at a Time

This section is a chain of reasoning, not four separate topics. Each part sets up the next, so read it in order.

2a · The Controversy

Two Legitimate Sides

For recognition: if a company's credit risk rises, its liabilities really are worth less. In a liquidation, that shortfall falls on someone other than equity holders, so the gain reflects genuine economics.

Against recognition: the IASB itself calls the resulting gain/loss "counter-intuitive" and "potentially misleading." The accounting may be correct, yet still confuse the very users it's meant to inform.

2b · Why Misinterpretation Is Predictable → H1

Labels Carry Baggage

Prior psychology and accounting research shows that presentation format and labeling shape interpretation. A "gain" carries a lifetime of prior association with good news, and users default to that prior unless given a strong reason not to.

2c · Disclosure as a Fix → H2

Basic vs. Relational Disclosure

Basic disclosure (the norm in practice at the time): states the amount of the gain/loss and that it relates to credit risk, but never says which direction.

Relational disclosure: explicitly states the direction, for example "this gain is due to a decrease in the Company's credit standing." Only this version targets the actual source of confusion.

2d · The Harder Prediction → H3

Losing Good News Is Easier Than Gaining It

Psychology research finds negative information is weighted more heavily and is harder to reverse than positive information. Downgrading a "gain = good news" impression should be easier than upgrading a "loss = bad news" impression to positive, so relational disclosures should work better for gains than for losses.

The Chain, Made Explicit

H1

The Problem Exists

Users will more often incorrectly identify the company with worsening (improving) credit risk as having lower (higher) risk.

H2

A Fix Is Proposed

Relational disclosures, stating the direction of the credit risk change, will improve users' ability to correctly interpret the gain or loss.

H3

The Fix Has Limits

The improvement from relational disclosures will be greater for gains (bad news in disguise) than for losses (good news in disguise).

Preview: What H3 Predicted, In Actual Numbers
Out of every 100 CPAs, how many correctly picked the lower-risk company?
When a Gain Was Reported
Basic Disclosure26 of 100
Relational Disclosure60 of 100
When a Loss Was Reported
Basic Disclosure29 of 100
Relational Disclosure41 of 100
Better disclosure helped in both cases. But look closely: when a gain was reported, correct answers more than doubled (26 to 60). When a loss was reported, correct answers barely rose (29 to 41). That gap is the asymmetry H3 predicted.
Reflective Questions
💬 On the Recognition Debate

Both sides of the credit-risk controversy have a legitimate point: the accounting can be economically correct and still be misleading in practice. Is it standard-setters' job to worry about how users interpret correct accounting, or only to get the economics right and leave interpretation to the user?

Conclusion · pp. 132–133 · ~3 min

Disclosure Helped, But Was Not Enough

The conclusion indicates that disclosure was not sufficient, and suggests that perhaps standard setters should consider excluding credit risk gains and losses from the income statement.

The finding, restated plainly
Even with the best disclosure tested, roughly half of participants still selected the higher-risk company as lower risk. Relational disclosures helped, but did not eliminate the misinterpretation.
Gaynor, McDaniel & Yohn (2011), Conclusion, p. 133
The Authors' Argument: Recognition vs. Disclosure
Disclosure

Footnote disclosures, even well-designed ones, could not fully overcome the misleading effect of where the gain or loss was presented and how it was labeled on the primary income statement.

Recognition

The authors argue this points towards excluding credit-risk gains and losses from the income statement altogether, since users rely on recognized numbers more heavily than footnote disclosures.

SFRS(I) 9 Today

This is essentially what SFRS(I) 9 (para. 5.7.7) implemented as the own-credit-risk component of a fair value liability change is presented in OCI, not profit or loss, unless doing so creates or enlarges an accounting mismatch. (Note that this treatment applies only to liabilities designated as at fair value through profit or loss under the fair value option, not to liabilities that are mandatorily measured at fair value through profit or loss, such as most derivative liabilities.)

Reflective Questions
💬 Discussion Question: Bringing It All Together

This paper found that footnotes alone did not fully fix the confusion from the fair value measurement of liabilities. The approach in SFRS(I) 9 moved such gain or loss out of profit or loss and into OCI instead. Do you think just moving it to OCI actually solves the problem, or does it just put the same problem somewhere fewer people will notice? What would you have suggested instead?

Before Class

Three Things to Know Without Your Notes

If you can explain these clearly in your own words, you are ready for the discussion.

01

Why a fair value gain on a liability signals worsening, not improving, credit risk, explained mechanically, not just recited.

02

The logical chain H1 → H2 → H3: misinterpretation exists, relational disclosure helps, and the help is asymmetric, plus why psychology predicts that asymmetry.

03

How SFRS(I) 9's OCI treatment for own-credit-risk changes is a direct standard-setting response to the exact problem this paper documents.

Terminology note. The paper refers to the U.S. GAAP "Fair Value Option" (SFAS No. 159). The Singapore equivalent is the fair value option for financial liabilities in SFRS(I) 9 para. 4.2.2, with the credit-risk presentation issue addressed specifically in SFRS(I) 9 para. 5.7.7 to 5.7.9. The behavioral finding and underlying accounting mechanics are the same across both standards.

If the Number Looks Too Good, Ask Why

Seventy percent of trained CPAs read a company's own bad news as good news. Come to class ready to explain why, and to say whether moving the item off the income statement was the right fix, or just a quieter place to hide the same confusion.
